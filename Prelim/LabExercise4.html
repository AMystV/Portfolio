Titanfall 2 Revisit

Archive of a Past Masterpiece

Respawn is still a relatively new studio, but its legacy can be found in the modern first person shooter in all its forms - from Medal of Honor to Modern Warfare and then, finally, to Titanfall. There's a through line there that's clear to see when you embark on Titanfall 2's brief but sumptuous campaign. The heritage of a studio that effectively created the cinematic shooter, that married that with spectacle and storytelling in Modern Warfare, and now has been afforded the opportunity to build an FPS campaign around the rarest of things in this genre - ideas.

For five or so hours, Respawn assaults you with concepts, with gameplay systems and with strokes of design ingenuity, never relenting until you hit the end credits. And then it's done. A close friend of mine, having finished the single-player, described it as a mic-drop (a line so good I was furious I hadn't thought of it myself) - it's Respawn's statement on what it is capable of as a studio, and just how far the first-person shooter campaign has been going in recent years.

Most of the time, Titanfall 2 feels like a set of mechanics in search of a game. To be fair, occasionally it finds one. There's a few points in the game — such as a level demanding platforming through a series of rotating landscapes being "assembled" for a maniacal robot's combat trials, or navigating through the bowels of a communications facility that wasn't designed for human interaction — where it finds its footing, no pun intended. It might be surprising to hear that Titanfall 2 is the most fun when you're doing the least shooting — when Respawn lets it feel more more like, say Portal , it's considerably more fun.

Campaign

The single player campaign alone is good enough to justify the purchase price--- though it's important to note that I'm the sort of gamer who prefers shorter, high-quality campaigns over sprawling open world slogs filled with busy work. Every mission has some twist and some clever design element that keeps the entire campaign diverse and fresh. Much of it is so good that I'm tempted to jump right back in and play it all again---and I'll do just that once the madness of the holiday season slows down and I have the time.

The characters are largely generic. Jack Cooper, the protagonist, even sounds generic. But his relationship with the Titan, BT 7274, is actually pretty moving. Their many humorous interactions are a nice touch as well. As for villains, you get boss fights, all of which play out as mech battles, but the actual bad guys remain pretty two-dimensional. It's not a bad story by any means, but I hope that the next game gives us more to latch onto.

Multiplayer

Bounty Hunt: You're rewarded for killing the enemy Pilots and AI, and then have to deposit your reward funds into a bank at the end of each wave. You can steal some of the money if you kill an enemy Pilot.

Amped Hardpoint: Teams battle to control points. If you control the point and then "amp" it up, you get even more points.

Pilots vs. Pilots: Just like it reads. You fight without Titans getting in the way.

Capture the Flag: Get the flag. Run with it. You've played this before.

Free For All: Shoot everybody. Nobody is your friend.

Last Titan Standing: Titans only, no Pilots allowed.

Skirmish: Basically Attrition with a lower score limit and no AI enemies. Still lots of fun, but just your basic Team Deathmatch (with mecha.)

I recommend playing the solo campaign before you move on to the multiplayer, as it serves as a great tutorial for PvP. You go through all SIX Titans in the campaign and get to try out a bunch of cool abilities. These Titans are:

Ion: The starting Titan class, but still a really great mech with fun powers. It's kind of the "every-man" or "every-Titan" with a good balanced loadout that includes rockets and a really stellar Laser Core ultimate ability.

Scorch: As the name implies, this one is all about fire. You can slam the ground and send a wave of liquid fire across the ground. The ultimate is a huge inferno.

Tone: Tracking missiles, a strong canon, and the wonderful Missile Core ultimate make Tone a really fun Titan to play. Missile Core is one of my favorite ultimates, though Northstar's version is better.

Ronin: A lighter, faster mech with a shotgun and a teleport ability, Ronin is a blast to play. This is the one with the Sword Core ability, giving you some crazy close-up lethality.

Northstar: Instead of Missile Core, Northstar has Flight Core. This is the one "flying" mech though it really just hovers above the ground. Think Pharah if you're an Overwatch player.

Legion: The last mech you get in the game is also super powerful. The chaingun is powerful, but the Smart Core ultimate makes it doubly so, since it targets enemies without having to aim (think Soldier 76 if you play Overwatch, but huge and can target way more stuff at once.)

Graphics And Gameplay

is what you'd expect for the most part. It builds off the previous game. You wall-run, slide and double-jump, and the more you do these things the faster you'll go. Grappling hooks in multiplayer simply extend your reach. In the campaign, you'll do a surprising amount of this stuff as well, as each level is designed around these core mechanics. Shooting feels a great deal like Call of Duty, unsurprisingly, though Respawn has done a great job creating really unique weapons. The arsenal is almost all fun to play, but certain weapons like the EVA-8 auto-shotgun are a blast.

Lots of little things make the game just feel good. Even just hopping into or out of your Titan is such a breeze. It just clicks nicely, in the same way a game like Metal Gear Solid V had very tight controls that simply felt good to use.

Titanfall 2 looks outstanding on both PS4 and PC, the two platforms I've played it on. I hear it looks very good on Xbox One as well, but haven't had the chance to try it out myself. Not only do the graphics look good, but in both single and multiplayer there's tons of level and map variety. You'll find yourself in all kinds of different environments, and they all look incredible. The campaign in particular has many jaw-dropping moments of raw beauty, but even the multiplayer is a looker.
